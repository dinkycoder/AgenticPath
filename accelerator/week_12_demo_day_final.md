## FINAL WORDS FROM EACH FACULTY MEMBER

### RAPHAEL LAUFER — Product
*"Here's the thing most people don't know about product demo day: the product doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be honest. Show what you've built, know what you're still building, and make investors feel that you're the person who will build the rest of it. Don't hide the roadmap — show it like a founder who is completely clear on what comes next and completely certain they can get there.*

*One last check from me: Before Demo Day, personally walk through your most important user journey — from landing page to completing a lesson — one more time. Fresh eyes. As if you're a stranger. Fix anything that feels off, no matter how small. That's the kodawari. That's the difference."*

---

### NINA FARKACHE — UX Research
*"The best thing you did in this program was talk to real users. Most founders skip that. They build for imaginary people and wonder why real people don't show up.*

*My last advice: when you're on stage, speak as if your ILP — your learner — is in the room. Because they might be. The investors in the room are evaluating whether you understand people. When you talk about a specific learner story, a real insight from an interview, a decision you changed because of something a user told you — that's when you become credible. Not just as a builder. As someone who builds for people.*

*One last check from me: Look at your pitch deck. Is there at least one real learner quote on it? Not a generic testimonial. A specific, named (or anonymized), real quote that shows you've been listening."*

---

### YUVAL GEFFEN — HCI + Investor
*"I'm going to give you the investor advice and the designer advice simultaneously, because they're actually the same advice.*

*The investor advice: Investors don't fund products. They fund trajectories. What they're evaluating is not where you are — it's the rate of change. How fast are you learning? How fast are you iterating? How fast are you growing? If your Week 12 traction is better than your Week 9 traction in a way that tells a clear story — that trajectory is your best pitch.*

*The designer advice: On Demo Day, every second of your demo is a design decision. The scenario you choose, the decision you ask the audience to make, the visual they see when the choice resolves — design it like a product. Intentionally. Every beat.*

*One last check from me: Run your 90-second demo scenario. Does it feel inevitable — like every beat was supposed to happen exactly that way? If there's any moment that feels awkward, unnatural, or rushed — fix that moment."*

---

### JORDANA DUMONT — Retention & User Behavior
*"Here's what I've learned studying people over many years: the thing people remember is not the information they received. It's how they felt when they received it.*

*On Demo Day, investors will forget most of what you say. They will remember how you made them feel. Did you make them feel the urgency of the problem? Did you make them feel the excitement of what you've built? Did you make them feel confident that you can execute?*

*Emotion is not fluff. It is the mechanism by which information becomes memorable. The best pitches I've ever heard were emotionally honest — founders who clearly cared deeply about the problem they were solving, who were genuinely excited by what they'd built, and who communicated that excitement without performing it.*

*One last check from me: Read your opening line out loud. Does it make YOU feel something? If not, it won't make the investor feel something. Rewrite it until it does."*

---

### CHRISTINA LANG — Growth & Community
*"The pitch ends and then the real work begins. Here's what most founders forget: Demo Day is a lead generation event, not a closing event. You don't close a seed round on stage. You start relationships on stage that you close over the next 90 days.*

*So your job at Demo Day is to be remembered. One specific, vivid thing about your pitch that stays with every investor in the room after they've seen 20 other pitches. For KodaWari — that thing should be the live demo moment where you put the investor inside a KodaWari lesson. Nobody else will do that. Make it unforgettable.*

*One last check from me: Post about Demo Day publicly — before it happens AND after. 'I'm pitching KodaWari at the Base44 Demo Day this Thursday in NYC. If multi-agent AI mastery matters to you — I'd love for you to be in the room.' Build the audience for your pitch before you give it. The room + the LinkedIn/Twitter live audience = 10x your reach."*

---

### JASON WARNKE — Enterprise
*"You've spent 12 weeks building a B2C product with B2B potential. My last advice to you is: don't forget the enterprise story in the room.*

*Investors at Demo Day are often from enterprise backgrounds. They think in terms of organizational buying, not individual subscriptions. When you say 'KodaWari has a $50,000/year enterprise licensing model for L&D teams' — I guarantee at least one investor in the room will lean forward. That's their world. Speak to them for one sentence.*

*You don't need a separate enterprise pitch. You need one line that acknowledges the enterprise dimension exists and is real: 'On the B2B side, we have [X] enterprise leads in pipeline at an average deal size of $15K–$30K/year.' One line. That's enough for me to know you've thought about it.*

*One last check from me: Is that one line in your pitch? If not, add it to Slide 4 (Traction) or Slide 6 (Business Model). One line."*

---

### J. SKYLER FERNANDES — Investment
*"I'm going to give you the most important advice I give every founder I work with, and it almost never lands the way I intend it. But I'll try one more time.*

*Stop optimizing your pitch. Start believing your story.*

*The founders who raise money are not always the ones with the best decks or the most polished demos. They're the ones who, when they stand up and talk about what they're building, make everyone in the room feel that this was inevitable. That this person was always going to build this thing. That the world needs it and they are the right one to bring it.*

*You have spent 12 weeks building evidence that KodaWari is real and that you can execute. Now stop thinking about the pitch and start thinking about why this matters to you personally. What is the world you want to see that KodaWari creates? What kind of builders do you want to make possible? When you stand up on that stage — say that. In your words. From that place.*

*One last check from me: Read me the last paragraph of your pitch. The ask. Is it a transaction — or is it an invitation? Investors write checks into people they believe in. Give me a reason to believe in you, not just your metrics."*
