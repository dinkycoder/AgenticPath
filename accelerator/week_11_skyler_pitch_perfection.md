## SESSION 1 (Monday) — PITCH DECONSTRUCTION

### 1. The 5 Laws of Demo Day Pitches

After watching hundreds of pitches at Demo Day events — YC Demo Day, TechStars, and the events I run at VU — here are the 5 laws that separate the pitches investors remember from the ones they forget before the next presenter walks on stage.

**Law 1: The first 30 seconds determine everything.**
In the first 30 seconds, investors decide whether to lean in or mentally check out. Your hook cannot be "Hi, I'm [name] and I'm building [product]." That is the opening of a forgettable pitch. Your first 30 seconds must create urgency, tension, or a provocative insight that makes the investor think: *"I need to know more about this."*

KodaWari's hook must open on the PROBLEM, not the founder or product. Make the investor feel the gap before you show them the bridge.

**Law 2: Show, don't tell. Always.**
Every time you are tempted to say "our platform is unique because..." — stop. Show it. Run the demo. Let the product speak. "Unique" is one of the most overused and least believed words in founder pitches. Demonstrating uniqueness takes 90 seconds. Claiming it takes 90 seconds and costs you credibility.

**Law 3: Numbers only exist if they're real.**
Investors are pattern-matching at high speed. They have seen 1,000 pitches with made-up projections. The moment they sense your numbers are speculative, your credibility for the entire pitch collapses. Only put numbers on your slides that you can defend in detail. "We project 50,000 users in Year 1" with nothing backing it up is worse than saying "We have 200 users today and here's our growth rate."

**Law 4: The ask must be specific and tied to milestones.**
"We're raising $750K" is weak. "We're raising $750K to hire 2 curriculum architects, complete 3 course tracks, and reach $25K MRR — which is our Series A threshold — within 6 months of close" is strong. Every dollar in your ask should be tied to a specific outcome. This shows investors their money has a job.

**Law 5: Confidence is not loudness. It is precision.**
The most confident pitches I've seen are not the most energetic ones. They are the most precise ones. A founder who can answer every question with a specific fact, a specific number, a specific story — radiates more confidence than one who speaks loudly and moves fast. Precision is preparation. Preparation is confidence.

### 2. The KodaWari Pitch — Full Review

Let's go through your 9-slide deck word by word. Here is the review framework I apply to every slide:

**For each slide, I ask:**
1. **What is the ONE thing this slide must communicate?** If there's more than one thing, cut.
2. **Is there any word or number that I can't immediately verify or defend?** If yes, cut or source it.
3. **Does this slide make the NEXT slide feel inevitable?** Great pitch decks have momentum. Each slide creates a question that the next slide answers.
4. **Would removing this slide hurt the pitch?** If not — remove it.

**Common KodaWari pitch mistakes I anticipate:**

*On the Problem slide:* Founders often make the problem too general. "AI education is broken" is not a problem statement — it's a category observation. Your problem must be: who, specifically, is suffering, from what, right now, and why the current solutions fail them. Get as specific as possible.

*On the Traction slide:* The temptation is to show impressive-sounding numbers out of context. "500 learners" sounds better as "500 learners with a 47% completion rate vs. the industry average of 15%." Context is everything. Relative numbers are almost always more compelling than absolute ones.

*On the Market slide:* The $400B EdTech TAM is too broad and investors will dismiss it. Build your market sizing from the bottom up: 500K engineers deploying multi-agent systems now × 10% addressable in year 1 × $199/year = $9.95M Year 1 revenue potential from B2C alone. That's credible. That's specific. That's how I think about market size.

*On the Team slide:* Most founders undersell themselves here because they feel they don't have "enough" experience. You don't need a McKinsey pedigree. You need a credible answer to: "Why does your background make you uniquely suited to build this?" Your personal connection to the problem is the most powerful thing on this slide.

### 3. The Demo — Precision Rehearsal

Your 90-second live demo scenario is the heart of your pitch. By Week 11, this must be absolutely airtight. Here is the rehearsal protocol:

**The 10x Rule:** Practice your demo scenario 10 times before Demo Day. Not think about it — physically run through it, out loud, with the product open in front of you. By the 10th time, you should be able to run it in your sleep, with one hand, on a bad WiFi connection, with a question from the audience mid-demo.

**The Failure Protocol:** What happens if the product breaks during your demo? You need a backup. Options:
1. A screen-recorded video backup that you can play instantly
2. A series of static screenshots that tell the same story
3. A practiced verbal walkthrough that describes the experience so vividly the audience can almost see it

Investors have seen products break in demos. They don't penalize you for it. They DO penalize you for panicking. Having a backup ready and deploying it smoothly = you're in control.

**The Audience Participation Moment:** The most memorable demos involve the audience. Consider asking one investor: "Can you make a decision for me? You're the AI architect. Your rogue agent is in sector 4. Do you isolate it, observe it, or shut it down?" Let them choose. The narrative branches based on their answer. They are now inside KodaWari. They will not forget that.

## SESSION 2 (Mid-Week) — LIVE INVESTOR SIMULATION

*This session is a live Q&A. You deliver your full 6-minute pitch, then I ask you the following questions in sequence. Answer each one live, without preparation time.*

### The 15 Questions I Will Ask

**On the business:**
1. "Walk me through your unit economics. What does it cost you to acquire a learner, and what's their lifetime value?"
2. "What does your MRR growth look like week over week for the last 8 weeks? Show me the curve."
3. "You mentioned a 40%+ completion rate. How are you measuring that, and what's the denominator?"
4. "What's your biggest enterprise pilot opportunity right now? Where is it in the process?"

**On the market:**
5. "Your TAM is $20B. What's your SAM — the portion you can actually reach in the next 3 years?"
6. "Why is now the right time for this? What's changed in the last 12 months that makes this urgent?"
7. "Walk me through your 3 most direct competitors and why each of them can't build what you're building."

**On the product:**
8. "What's the single biggest product risk right now? What could make this not work?"
9. "If I gave you the $750K today — what's the first thing you'd hire for, and why?"
10. "What have you learned from your learners in the last 30 days that changed how you think about the product?"

**On the founder:**
11. "Why are you the right person to build this? Specifically."
12. "What has been the hardest moment in building KodaWari so far? How did you handle it?"
13. "What do you know about multi-agent AI and the education market that most people don't?"

**On the deal:**
14. "You're raising at a $5M cap. Walk me through how you arrived at that valuation."
15. "What does your cap table look like? Any investors already in?"

After each answer, I will push back once. You must hold your ground, or concede gracefully with: "That's fair — here's what I'm watching for that would validate or invalidate that."
